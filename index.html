I chose Option 1: AI Transparency & Trust.
Claro's AI surfaces recommendations, but fewer than 8% of users act on them. This case study explores why.

01 Problem Framing & Research

From assumption to design brief

Before designing anything, the brief's core claim needs to be stress-tested. "Users don't trust recommendations" is a conclusion — not a problem statement. Trust is an outcome. Something upstream causes it to break, and different root causes demand completely different design responses.

Product owner assumption
"Users don't trust the recommendations."

This could be accurate. But it could also be masking three entirely different explanations — each pointing to a different design solution.

ALT 01
Relevance failure
Recommendations are simply bad. Users aren't skeptical — the suggestions don't apply to their financial situation at all.
↳ Needs data to rule out
ALT 02
Timing failure
Right recommendation, wrong moment. A subscription switch nudge at 9am on a commute gets ignored regardless of trust level.
↳ Needs data to rule out
ALT 03
Trust failure
Users reach the card, read it, and don't act because the AI's reasoning is invisible. "I don't know why it's telling me this."
↳ Confirmed by qualitative

User interviews provide the confirming signal. The specific language people reach for — "I don't know why it's telling me this" and "what if it's wrong?" — maps directly to transparency and uncertainty anxiety. These are trust failure patterns, not relevance or timing complaints.

The first drop — sent to opened — is assumed to be large but expected. Notification abandonment in this range is typical for fintech push campaigns. Users haven't seen the product yet; this is a copy problem, not a design one.

The assumed critical failure is stage three: card viewed to CTA tapped. If users are making it this far — opening the app, finding the card, reading it — something on the card itself is failing to convert a willing reader into an actor. That is the primary design surface, and it aligns with what qualitative interviews confirm.

Verified problem statement
Users reach the recommendation card, read it, and don't act — because the card fails to provide enough visible reasoning to overcome natural skepticism about AI making decisions about their money.
Reasoning must be visible by default
Not tucked behind a "why?" tap. The first thing a user reads should be the data that generated the recommendation — not just the recommendation itself.
Confidence must be qualitative, not numerical
Showing "87% confidence" increases anxiety, not trust. Users need a human signal — "based on 6 months of spending data" lands better than a percentage score.
Dispute must be a first-class action
Users who disagree currently have nowhere to go. A dead end at disagreement kills trust further. The AI must appear teachable, not authoritative.
Trust compounds across interactions
A user who acts on one recommendation and sees a good outcome is far more likely to act on the next. The card must close the feedback loop visibly over time.

04 AI-Assisted Process

How AI was used — and what it got wrong

Two instances where Claude directly shaped this work. The full prompts are shown. So is the gap between what the AI produced and what actually ended up in the design.

INSTANCE 01 Hypothesis generation before designing
Prompt
The brief says "users don't trust the recommendations. Engagement is under 8%." Before I design anything, I want to interrogate this assumption. What are the competing hypotheses for why engagement is low — and what data would help me distinguish between them? Context: personal finance app, AI-generated recommendations, mobile-first, users range from financially aware to avoidant.
Raw output
Claude returned four hypotheses: (1) relevance failure — recommendations don't match user needs, (2) timing failure — right recommendation, wrong moment, (3) trust failure — users don't understand the AI's reasoning, (4) UX friction — the interaction is too high-effort. For each, it suggested distinguishing data: click-through rates by recommendation type, session timing, qualitative interview language, funnel drop-off analysis.
Critique
Useful structure, but generic — applicable to any product, not specifically to personal finance. The output didn't account for the psychological weight of money decisions. A user who "doesn't trust" a Netflix recommendation skips it. A user who "doesn't trust" a recommendation about their bank account may actively distrust the whole app. Claude treated both as equivalent. The fintech-specific escalation of trust failure was absent entirely.
What changed
The four-hypothesis framework became three — relevance, timing, trust — with fintech context applied to each. UX friction was folded into trust failure because in personal finance, friction is usually anxiety, not effort. This became the three alt cards in Section 01. The AI gave me a scaffold; I supplied the domain knowledge.
INSTANCE 02 Pressure-testing the dispute interaction
Prompt
I'm designing the dispute flow for an AI recommendation card in a personal finance app. A user taps "This doesn't seem right." Walk me through every state from that tap to resolution. What does the system show? What does the AI learn? Consider three user types: (a) the user is right and the data is wrong, (b) the user is right but the timing is wrong, (c) the user is wrong but won't accept being told so.
Raw output
Claude described a multi-step flow: tap → reason selection → AI shows its full reasoning → user confirms or escalates → model update confirmation reading "Thanks for helping Claro improve." It suggested five reason categories and a "teach the AI" framing throughout.
Critique
The flow was centred on ML feedback collection, not user resolution. "Thanks for helping Claro improve" treats a frustrated user as a data source rather than someone whose problem needs solving. Five reason categories adds cognitive load at exactly the moment the user is already skeptical. The "AI explains itself in response to a dispute" step is backwards — showing more AI output to a disbelieving user deepens distrust, it doesn't resolve it.
What changed
The dispute flow was rebuilt around user resolution. Three reason options, not five. The AI doesn't counter-explain — it acknowledges and closes. The confirmation reads "Got it, we'll update your recommendations" not a training-data thank-you. Model feedback happens invisibly. The result is the dispute state in Section 05.

06 Decision Journal

Five decisions, annotated

The choices that had the most downstream consequences. Each could have gone a different way — the rationale is the design.

D–01Show reasoning inline, not behind a tap
Alternatives
Progressive disclosure (tap to expand), tooltip on confidence badge, separate detail page
Why this one
The trust failure is at point-of-reading. A user who's already skeptical won't tap to learn more — they'll dismiss. Reasoning must be present before they decide to leave.
What I'd test
Whether visible reasoning increases CTA conversion, or just increases card dwell-time without converting. The latter would mean the card needs restructuring, not just more data.
D–02Qualitative confidence label, not a percentage score
Alternatives
"87% confident", star rating (3/5), traffic light (green/amber/red), no confidence indicator at all
Why this one
"87% confident" reads as "13% wrong" to a skeptical user managing their money. Qualitative framing anchors on evidence ("6 months of data"), which is harder to argue with than a probability.
What I'd test
A/B: "High confidence" vs "Based on your last 6 months" vs "87% match." Which drives the highest CTA rate among users who previously dismissed a recommendation?
D–03Bottom sheet for dispute, not a full-screen modal
Alternatives
Full-screen takeover, inline expansion of the card, a separate feedback page in settings
Why this one
Bottom sheet preserves context — the user can still see the card they're disputing. A full-screen modal feels punishing for what should be a lightweight action. Inline expansion would force the card to grow to a disruptive size.
What I'd test
Completion rate on the dispute flow. If users open the sheet and close it without selecting a reason, the three options don't reflect real mental models. That's a qualitative research trigger.
D–04Dispute resolution is user-centred, not ML-centred
Alternatives
"Thanks for helping Claro improve" confirmation, showing AI counter-reasoning, multi-step follow-up questions for the model
Why this one
A frustrated user is not a data source — they're someone whose problem needs resolving. ML feedback happens invisibly in the background. The UI acknowledges the user, not the model.
What I'd test
Whether users who complete a dispute return and engage with the next recommendation. A successful dispute flow should increase long-term engagement, not just reduce churn at this card.
D–05Track record module only appears after 3+ accepted recommendations
Alternatives
Show it from day one, never show it, show it after the first recommendation, show it in a separate history section
Why this one
A new user has no history to reference — "0 of 0 suggestions worked" actively undermines trust. The module only earns its place when there's a meaningful track record to show. Before then, inline reasoning carries the weight.
What I'd test
Whether 3 accepted recommendations is the right threshold — or if outcome-based framing ("you saved RM90 following Claro's advice") would be more persuasive than a completion count.
