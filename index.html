01 The starting point

Interrogating the assumption

Before designing anything, the brief's core claim needs to be stress-tested. "Users don't trust recommendations" is a conclusion — not a problem statement. Trust is an outcome. Something upstream causes it to break, and different root causes demand completely different design responses.

Product owner assumption
"Users don't trust the recommendations."

This could be accurate. But it could also be masking three entirely different explanations — each pointing to a different design solution.

ALT 01
Relevance failure
Recommendations are simply bad. Users aren't skeptical — the suggestions don't apply to their financial situation at all.
↳ Needs data to rule out
ALT 02
Timing failure
Right recommendation, wrong moment. A subscription switch nudge at 9am on a commute gets ignored regardless of trust level.
↳ Needs data to rule out
ALT 03
Trust failure
Users reach the card, read it, and don't act because the AI's reasoning is invisible. "I don't know why it's telling me this."
↳ Confirmed by qualitative

User interviews provide the confirming signal. The specific language people reach for — "I don't know why it's telling me this" and "what if it's wrong?" — maps directly to transparency and uncertainty anxiety. These are trust failure patterns, not relevance or timing complaints.

"I don't know why it's telling me this. Like, what data is it even looking at?"

"What if it's wrong? I'm not going to switch my phone plan because an app told me to."

The first drop — sent to opened — is large but expected. 58% notification abandonment sits within the normal range for fintech push campaigns. Users haven't seen the product yet; this is a copy problem, not a design one.

The critical failure is stage three: card viewed to CTA tapped. These users showed intent. They opened the app, found the card, and read it. Something on that card is failing to convert a willing reader into an actor. That is the primary design surface for this sprint.

03 Synthesis

From assumption to design brief

Combining funnel data with qualitative interview signal produces a precise, testable problem statement — specific enough to design against, measurable enough to evaluate against.

Verified problem statement
Users reach the recommendation card, read it, and don't act — because the card fails to provide enough visible reasoning to overcome natural skepticism about AI making decisions about their money.

This reframing matters for three reasons. It localises the design problem to a specific screen. It identifies the mechanism — absence of reasoning, not absence of relevance. And it suggests a measurable outcome: increase in card-viewed-to-CTA-tapped conversion rate. Everything else is secondary to fixing that card.

01
Reasoning must be visible by default
Not tucked behind a "why?" tap. The first thing a user reads should be the data that generated the recommendation — not just the recommendation itself.
02
Confidence must be qualitative, not numerical
Showing "87% confidence" increases anxiety, not trust. Users need a human signal — "based on 6 months of spending data" lands better than a percentage score.
03
Dispute must be a first-class action
Users who disagree currently have nowhere to go. A dead end at disagreement kills trust further. The AI must appear teachable, not authoritative.
04
Trust compounds across interactions
A user who acts on one recommendation and sees a good outcome is far more likely to act on the next. The card must close the feedback loop visibly over time.
